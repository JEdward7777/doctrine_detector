The Roman practice of crucifixion had a profound impact on the perception of the cross in early Christianity. Crucifixion was a brutal and humiliating form of execution reserved for the worst criminals, slaves, and rebels in the Roman Empire. The Romans used it as a means to instill fear and deter others from committing crimes.

However, in the Christian tradition, the cross took on a new and profound significance. It became a symbol of sacrifice, redemption, and victory over sin and death. This transformation was primarily due to the crucifixion of Jesus Christ, who willingly endured the cross to atone for the sins of humanity.

The Apostle Paul captured this sentiment when he wrote in 1 Corinthians 1:18, "For the message of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God."
