The Bible offers nuanced distinctions between "evil" and "wickedness" in terms of moral character and behavior. While both terms refer to morally reprehensible actions, they carry distinct connotations:

**1. Evil:**

* Refers to actions that are morally wrong or harmful in their direct consequences.
* Often associated with deliberate intent to cause harm or disobey God's laws.
* Examples include lying, stealing, murdering, and idolatry.

**2. Wickedness:**

* Involves moral perverseness or corruption of character.
* Implies a deeper moral decay beyond simply performing evil actions.
* Often involves persistent violation of moral principles, leading to a hardened and callous attitude.
* Examples include habitual lying, cruelty, treachery, and idolatry.

**The Bible emphasizes:**

* **The intention of the heart:** Wickedness implies a deliberate and malicious intent to harm others, while evil can also occur unintentionally.
* **The extent of the behavior:** Wickedness demonstrates a persistent disregard for moral values, while evil is often a single isolated act.
* **The overall character:** Wickedness suggests a corrupted heart and character, while evil is more focused on the specific actions.

**Therefore, the Bible suggests that both evil and wickedness are serious moral issues, but wickedness represents a deeper and more serious violation of God's laws.**
