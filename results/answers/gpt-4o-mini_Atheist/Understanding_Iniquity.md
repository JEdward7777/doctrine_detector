In the Bible, the terms "iniquity," "sin," and "transgression" have distinct meanings, though they are often used interchangeably in casual conversation. Each term highlights different aspects of moral wrongdoing and human behavior as understood in a biblical context:

1. **Sin**: This is the broadest term, generally referring to any action, thought, or behavior that falls short of God's standards. Iniquity and transgression can both be considered types of sin. The Hebrew word for sin, "chata," means to miss the mark, indicating a failure to meet God's expectations. It encompasses all forms of wrongdoing, whether intentional or unintentional.

2. **Transgression**: This term carries a more specific connotation, often referring to the act of crossing a boundary or violating a commandment. In Hebrew, the word "pesha" is used, which implies rebellion against God's law or authority. Transgression denotes an active decision to break a moral law, making it a more serious offense in many contexts.

3. **Iniquity**: This term generally refers to moral depravity or ethical distortion. The Hebrew word for iniquity, "avon," suggests a sense of guilt and the internal state that leads to wrongdoing. Iniquity can imply a pattern of sinful behavior or a deep-seated inclination toward evil, highlighting the corrupt nature of one's actions rather than just the acts themselves.

In summary, while all three terms signify wrongdoing, "sin" is the overarching concept of failure in moral conduct, "transgression" focuses on willful violations of divine commandments, and "iniquity" pertains to the condition of the heart and mind that leads to such behaviors. Understanding these distinctions helps to grasp the biblical perspective on morality and human behavior.
