Baptism is a significant rite in Christian faith, and the Bible mentions several methods of baptism that have been interpreted differently across various Christian traditions. Here are the primary methods and their interpretations:

1. **Immersion**: This is the practice of fully submerging the person being baptized in water. The most prominent scriptural basis for immersion comes from passages such as Matthew 3:16, where Jesus is baptized by John in the Jordan River, and Acts 8:38-39, describing the baptism of the Ethiopian eunuch where they both went into the water. Churches that practice immersion, such as Baptists and many Pentecostal groups, often argue that it symbolizes the believer’s identification with the death, burial, and resurrection of Christ (Romans 6:4).

2. **Pouring (Affusion)**: This method involves pouring water over the head of the individual being baptized. While this specific action is not explicitly detailed in the New Testament, some interpret passages like Acts 10:47-48, where the Holy Spirit falls upon the Gentiles, as a case of believers being baptized by pouring and not immersion. Traditions such as Methodism and some Anglican and Lutheran denominations often practice pouring, emphasizing that the action is more about the symbolism of being cleansed.

3. **Sprinkling (Aspersion)**: This is the act of sprinkling water on the individual, which is often linked to ceremonial cleansing in the Old Testament (e.g., Hebrews 9:13). Some interpret Jesus’ teachings and the New Covenant's themes as demonstrating that physical immersion is not as critical to the sacrament as the spiritual reality it conveys. Many Reformed and some paedo-baptist traditions, where infants are baptized, may use sprinkling to signify the inclusion of infants into the covenant community.

4. **Symbolic Closures**: Although not a method of baptism in itself, some Christian communities interpret baptism more symbolically, viewing it as an outward sign rather than a requisite for salvation. For instance, some Quaker groups do not practice any form of baptism, believing that the experience of the Holy Spirit constitutes the real baptism, as illustrated in Matthew 3:11, where John references a different kind of baptism by the Holy Spirit.

### Variations in Interpretation:

- **Salvation and Sacramental Views**: Some traditions (like the Catholic Church and Orthodox churches) view baptism as a sacrament that brings about grace and is essential for salvation (John 3:5). In contrast, evangelicals may see baptism primarily as an act of obedience following a personal faith in Christ, thus interpreting it as symbolic rather than a means of grace.

- **Infant vs. Believer's Baptism**: This fundamental divide among denominations also leads to varying practices. Paedo-baptists (like Presbyterians) see baptism as a sign of inclusion in the covenant community, similar to circumcision in the Old Testament. In contrast, believer’s baptists argue that baptism should follow an individual’s conscious decision to follow Christ.

The theological understanding of baptism continues to be a source of rich discussion within Christianity, often reflecting broader views on salvation, grace, and community identity. Each tradition brings unique insights into the meaning and significance of this foundational Christian practice.
