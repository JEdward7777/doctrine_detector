In contrast, the practice of redeeming people or property in ancient Israel, as outlined in the Hebrew Scriptures (the Old Testament), was primarily rooted in the legal and social systems defined in the Mosaic Law. There are a couple of key aspects of this ancient practice:

If someone fell into debt and had to sell their land, the family of the seller had the right to redeem it when they were able to do so, ensuring that land remained within the family. The Book of Ruth, for example, showcases the practice of "kinsman-redeemer," where a relative is expected to rescue or redeem a family member in financial distress or to marry a widow to preserve family lineage.

While both concepts of redemption involve the ideas of restoration and recovery, biblical redemption (especially in the New Testament) focuses on personal, spiritual salvation and the relationship between God and humanity. In contrast, the ancient practices in Israel were more communal and legalistic, aimed at maintaining social order, family integrity, and economic justice. 

Both concepts, however, can be tied together through the overarching theme of God's mercy and the restoration of His people, whether in a spiritual or societal context.
