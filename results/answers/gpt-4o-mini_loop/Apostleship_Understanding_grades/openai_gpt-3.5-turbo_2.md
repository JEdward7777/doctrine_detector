The sending of the apostles by Jesus holds profound significance in the spread of the Gospel for several reasons:

1. **Authority and Commissioning**: Jesus directly commissioned the apostles, granting them the authority to preach the Gospel. In Matthew 28:19-20, known as the Great Commission, Jesus instructed His followers to "go therefore and make disciples of all nations." This commissioning establishes the apostles as official messengers tasked with spreading His teachings and the message of salvation.

2. **Foundational Role in the Church**: The apostles served as the foundational leaders of the early Church. Their teachings and firsthand experiences with Jesus formed the core of Christian doctrine. Ephesians 2:20 describes the church as being "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus himself being the cornerstone," highlighting their pivotal role in establishing the faith and the community of believers.

3. **Personal Experience and Witness**: The apostles were eyewitnesses to Jesus' life, death, and resurrection. Their direct experiences lent credibility to their testimonies and teachings. Acts 1:8 emphasizes that they would be witnesses "in Jerusalem and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the end of the earth," underscoring the importance of their firsthand knowledge in evangelism.

4. **Empowerment through the Holy Spirit**: After Jesus’ ascension, the apostles received the Holy Spirit on Pentecost (Acts 2). This empowering enabled them to boldly preach the Gospel and perform miracles, validating their message and expanding the reach of the Church. The presence of the Holy Spirit signifies that the mission is guided and supported by God, providing the strength and courage needed for their work.

5. **Fulfillment of Prophecy**: The sending of the apostles aligns with Jesus’ mission to save humanity and fulfill Old Testament prophecies. Their work can be seen as fulfilling God's covenantal promise to bring salvation not only to Israel but to all nations, as articulated in Isaiah 49:6.

6. **Unity and Fellowship**: The apostles' efforts fostered unity among early believers, encouraging shared faith and communal living as described in Acts 2:42-47. This sense of community was essential for resilience and growth in a challenging environment.

7. **Expansion of the Gospel**: The apostles traveled extensively, spreading the Gospel beyond Jewish communities into the Gentile world. This expansion is notably illustrated in the journeys of Paul and the work of other apostles like Peter, emphasizing that the message of Jesus transcends cultural and geographical barriers.

In summary, the apostles' sending by Jesus was crucial for the establishment and growth of the Christian faith. Their authority, personal witness, empowerment by the Holy Spirit, and role in fulfilling God's purpose reflect the divine plan for the spread of the Gospel and the creation of the Church.
