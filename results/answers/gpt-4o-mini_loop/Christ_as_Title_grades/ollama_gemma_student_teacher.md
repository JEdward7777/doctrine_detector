1. **Root Meaning**: "Christ" is derived from the Greek word "Christos," which translates the Hebrew "Mashiach" or "Messiah," meaning "anointed one." In the Old Testament, "Messiah" referred to anointed figures such as kings, priests, and prophets, but it also carried an eschatological expectation of a future deliverer for Israel.

2. **Initial Usage**: In the Gospels, especially in the Synoptic Gospels (Matthew, Mark, and Luke), "Christ" is closely tied to Jesus’ identity. Peter’s confession in Matthew 16:16 famously identifies Jesus as "the Christ, the Son of the living God." This acknowledgment signifies the recognition of Jesus as the ultimate fulfillment of Messianic prophecies given throughout the Old Testament.

3. **Titles and Roles**: Jesus is referred to as "the Christ," emphasizing His role as the awaited Messiah. As His ministry unfolds, the term embodies deeper theological meanings, portraying Him as Savior and Redeemer. These roles culminate in His sacrificial death and resurrection, which serve as the cornerstone of Christian faith.

4. **Post-Resurrection Understanding**: Following the resurrection, the understanding of "Christ" expands dramatically. In the Acts of the Apostles, the proclamation of Jesus as the Christ becomes central to apostolic preaching (e.g., Acts 2:36), marking the resurrection as a crucial validation of His divine identity and salvific work.

5. **Pauline Theology**: The apostle Paul frequently employs "Christ" in his letters, often coupling it with "Jesus" (e.g., Jesus Christ). He elaborates on the communal and cosmic implications of Christ, portraying Him as the head of the church (Ephesians 1:22-23) and as the source of salvation for both Jews and Gentiles (Romans 10:12-13). Paul’s teachings underscore the necessity of Christ’s atoning sacrifice and the redemption offered through faith in Him.

6. **Early Christian Identity**: For early Christians, "Christ" transcended mere title; it became integral to their identity. The "in Christ" language prevalent in Paul’s epistles reflects a profound relational aspect, indicating that believers are members of Christ’s body and recipients of His grace and resurrection life.

7. **Eschatological and Doctrinal Significance**: In later New Testament writings, such as Hebrews and Revelation, "Christ" assumes a more pronounced eschatological role, emphasizing His supremacy, sovereignty, and authority over all creation. In conclusion, the term "Christ" evolves from a title indicating a long-awaited figure to an essential element of Christian faith that encompasses Jesus’ identity, His role in salvation through atonement and redemption, and the believer's relationship with Him.
