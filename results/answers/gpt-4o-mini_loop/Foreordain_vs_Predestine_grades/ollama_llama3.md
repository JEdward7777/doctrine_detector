In Christian theology, the terms 'foreordain' and 'predestine' are closely related and often used interchangeably, though they can carry slightly different connotations depending on the context in which they are used.

1. **Foreordain**: This term generally means that God has determined or decided in advance certain events or outcomes. The concept of foreordination suggests that God, in His sovereignty, has a plan that encompasses the entirety of creation, including the history of salvation. This is reflected in Scripture, such as in Ephesians 1:11, which states that God "works all things according to the counsel of His will." This acknowledges God's comprehensive control over all aspects of existence.

2. **Predestine**: This term specifically refers to God's act of determining the fate of individuals with respect to salvation and eternal life. Predestination asserts that before the foundation of the world, God chose certain individuals to receive salvation. This idea is most notably articulated in passages like Romans 8:29-30, where Paul explains that those whom God foreknew were predestined to be conformed to the image of His Son. Predestination emphasizes God's initiative in the plan of salvation.

While both terms point to God's sovereign action in determining the course of history and individuals' destinies, 'foreordain' can refer more broadly to God's overall eternal plan, whereas 'predestine' is more specifically focused on the salvation of individuals.

The interpretation of these concepts varies among different Christian traditions. For example, Calvinism emphasizes God's sovereignty, asserting that God's choice is unconditional and that His grace is irresistible for the elect. This view posits that once God has chosen someone for salvation, that individual cannot lose that salvation. In contrast, Arminianism places a strong emphasis on human free will, teaching that God's predestination is based on His foreknowledge of who will choose to accept or reject His grace. According to this view, individuals have the ability to respond to God's offer of salvation.
