It historically refers to a ruler governing one-fourth of a territory, commonly associated with divisions of larger regions. During the time of Jesus, the term is primarily linked to the governance of specific areas within Judea, particularly under the Herodian dynasty.

Judea was under Roman rule at this time, but local governance was frequently entrusted to client kings and tetrarchs, offering a semblance of autonomy. Among the notable tetrarchs was Herod Antipas, who ruled over Galilee and Perea. He is mentioned in the New Testament for his significant roles, including the execution of John the Baptist and his interaction with Jesus during the latter's trial.

The title 'tetrarch' reveals a governance structure characterized by a balance of local authority and imperial oversight. This system allowed regional rulers like Antipas to exercise considerable influence while remaining subject to Rome's overarching power. Such a political climate facilitated the emergence of a diverse cultural landscape, where Jewish traditions were interwoven with Roman governance.
