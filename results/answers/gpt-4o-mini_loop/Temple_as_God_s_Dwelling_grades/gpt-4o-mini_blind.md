The concept of the temple as God's dwelling is a significant theme in both the Old and New Testaments, reflecting God's desire to be present among His people.

### Old Testament

1. **The Tabernacle**:
   - The initial structure representing God's presence among the Israelites was the Tabernacle, as detailed in Exodus (Exodus 25:8-9). It was a portable sanctuary designed to accompany the Israelites during their wanderings in the desert. God instructed Moses to construct it to house His presence, which was signified by the Shekinah glory, a visible manifestation of God’s holiness.

2. **Solomon's Temple**:
   - Eventually, King Solomon built a permanent temple in Jerusalem, which replaced the Tabernacle as the dwelling place of God (1 Kings 6). This majestic structure was the center of worship where sacrifices could be made. The Holy of Holies, the innermost chamber, was believed to be the dwelling place of God's presence (1 Kings 8:10-11). Solomon's dedication of the temple emphasized God's promise to dwell among His people (1 Kings 8:27).

3. **Symbolism and Prophetic Vision**:
   - The temple acted as a symbol of the covenant relationship between God and Israel, representing divine holiness and the conditions for worship. Prophets like Ezekiel highlighted both the glory of God departing from the temple due to Israel's sin (Ezekiel 10:18-19) and the future hope of God’s return (Ezekiel 43:1-5), pointing to a restorative promise for His people.

### New Testament

1. **Jesus as the Temple**:
   - In the New Testament, the concept of the temple shifts dramatically with the coming of Jesus Christ. In John 2:19-21, Jesus identifies His body as the temple, indicating that through His death and resurrection, He becomes the ultimate meeting place between God and humanity. This transformation signifies that God's presence is no longer confined to a physical structure but is embodied in Christ Himself.

2. **The Church as a Temple**:
   - The New Testament further develops this idea by teaching that believers collectively form the "temple of God." According to 1 Corinthians 3:16-17 and Ephesians 2:19-22, the church—composed of all believers—becomes the dwelling place of God through the Holy Spirit. This emphasizes that God’s presence is with His people regardless of location, highlighting the intimate nature of the relationship between God and His church.

3. **Eschatological Vision**:
   - The book of Revelation presents a future reality where God’s presence will be fully realized among His people. In Revelation 21:22, it is stated that there is no temple in the New Jerusalem because God and the Lamb (Jesus Christ) will be its temple, indicating a direct and unmediated presence of God among His redeemed people. This future hope assures believers of an eternal dwelling with God.

### Summary
The temple as God's dwelling evolves from the physical Tabernacle and Temple of the Old Testament to the person of Jesus Christ and the collective body of believers in the New Testament. This progression highlights the profound relationship that God longs to have with humanity, culminating in the promise of eternal communion with Him in the new creation. Through this journey, we see a beautiful revelation of God’s faithfulness and desire to dwell among His people throughout history.
