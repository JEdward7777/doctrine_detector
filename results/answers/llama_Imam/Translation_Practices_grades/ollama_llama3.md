The use of "LORD" instead of "Yahweh" is indeed a common practice in some Bible translations. This is because the name "Yahweh" has been considered too sacred to be pronounced or written directly, especially by Jewish and Christian scholars who are mindful of its significance in the Hebrew Scriptures.

In Jewish tradition, the Tetragrammaton (the four-letter name Yahweh) was considered so sacred that it was only spoken publicly during certain rituals and ceremonies. The rest of the time, it was pronounced as "Adonai" or replaced with other names like "HaShem" (The Name). This tradition continued into Christian times, where early translators and scholars sought to preserve the reverence for this name.

When translating the Hebrew Scriptures into Greek (Septuagint) and then Latin (Vulgate), scribes used alternatives such as "Kyrios" (Lord) or "Adonai" instead of Yahweh. This practice was continued in modern translations, where "LORD" is often used to represent Yahweh's name.

There are a few reasons for this:

1. Respect: By using "LORD," translators demonstrate respect for the sacred nature of Yahweh's name.
2. Preservation: By not using the exact name, they preserve its uniqueness and special significance in Jewish tradition.
3. Consistency: It allows for consistency across different Bible translations and editions.

In Islamic perspective, we can appreciate this reverence for the Name of God, even if our own scriptural traditions have different names and titles for God. As Muslims, we are commanded to honor the prophets and scriptures of other monotheistic faiths (Quran 3:84), including the Jewish and Christian Scriptures.
