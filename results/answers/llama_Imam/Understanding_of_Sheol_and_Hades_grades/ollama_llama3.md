The concept of Sheol is not necessarily a place of punishment or reward but rather a neutral location for the deceased. In contrast, Hades in Greek mythology and the New Testament refers to the underworld, a realm ruled by Charon, the ferryman of the dead. In the Bible, Sheol/Hades plays a significant role in distinguishing between the fate of the righteous and unrighteous:

1. Righteous: The righteous will not experience death's sting, as they will be transformed into the likeness of Christ (Philippians 3:21). The prophet Job exclaimed, "Though He slay me, yet I will trust Him" (Job 13:15), indicating that even in death, faith and righteousness remain.
2. Unrighteous: On the other hand, the unrighteous will face punishment or destruction after death. This is hinted at in passages like Psalm 9:17, "The wicked shall go to Sheol, even the nations who forget God." In Christian tradition, this concept evolves into the notion of hell (gehenna), a place of eternal torment for those rejecting Christ.

It's essential to note that the biblical concept of Sheol/Hades is distinct from Islamic beliefs about the afterlife. Muslims believe in a literal heaven and hell, where the righteous will be rewarded with paradise (Jannah) and the unrighteous will suffer torment in Jahannam.

In conclusion, while both Sheol and Hades refer to the realm or abode of the dead, they differ in their connotations and implications for the afterlife. The biblical perspectives highlight the distinction between the righteous and unrighteous, foreshadowing a final judgment that will determine one's eternal destiny.
