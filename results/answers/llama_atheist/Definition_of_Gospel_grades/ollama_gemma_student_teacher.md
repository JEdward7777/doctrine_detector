In the context of the Bible, particularly in the New Testament, the gospel refers to the message of salvation that Jesus Christ and his followers proclaimed. The four canonical Gospels – Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John – are considered the primary sources of this message.

In Christian theology, the gospel is understood as the account of Jesus' life, death, and resurrection, which is seen as the central event in human history. According to this narrative, Jesus, the Son of God, came to earth to redeem humanity from sin and its consequences. Through his teachings, miracles, and ultimate sacrifice on the cross, Jesus provided a means for people to be reconciled with God.

The gospel message is often summarized in terms of four key elements:

1. The person and ministry of Jesus Christ: His life, teachings, miracles, and death.
2. The significance of his crucifixion and resurrection: These events are seen as the means by which humans can be forgiven and have their sins washed away.
3. The call to repentance and faith: Believers are called to turn from their sinful ways and trust in Jesus' sacrifice for salvation.
4. The promise of new life and eternal salvation: Those who put their faith in Jesus receive the gift of eternal life and a new relationship with God.

Throughout the New Testament, various authors and epistles emphasize the importance of this gospel message as the foundation of Christian faith and practice. In Paul's letters, for example, he repeatedly emphasizes that the gospel is the "power of God" (Romans 1:16) and the means by which people can be justified before God (Romans 3:24-25).

In summary, the term "gospel" in the context of the Bible refers to the message of salvation that Jesus Christ and his followers proclaimed. This message is centered on the person and work of Jesus, highlighting his life, death, resurrection, and call to repentance and faith.
