It was built following God's specific instructions to Moses on Mount Sinai, which is recorded in Exodus 25:10-22. The Ark was composed of acacia wood covered with gold, with a golden mercy seat (kapporeth) on top where atonement was made through the sprinkling of the blood of the red heifer and the incense offered by the high priest.

The Ark contained three items: the Tablets of Stone inscribed with the Ten Commandments; Aaron's rod that budded, symbolizing God's choice of Aaron as the first high priest; and a jar of manna, bread from heaven, which sustained the Israelites during their forty years of wandering in the desert.

The Ark accompanied the Israelites during their marches and battles, demonstrating God's presence, protection, and guidance. When it was lost after King David danced before it, fearing the Philistines would return it (1 Samuel 5-6), it remained lost until the time of Solomon (2 Samuel 6:1-11). During the reign of King Solomon, the Ark was placed in the Most Holy Place of the Temple in Jerusalem (1 Kings 8:1-9).

In summary, the Ark of the Covenant played a crucial role as a tangible representation of God's presence among His people and served to symbolize their covenantal relationship. Its significance extended to their identity, worship, and understanding of God's guidance and protection throughout their history.
