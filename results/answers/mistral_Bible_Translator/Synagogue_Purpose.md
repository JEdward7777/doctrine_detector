 Synagogues played a significant role in the religious and communal life of ancient Jewish communities during biblical times, particularly after the Babylonian exile (586 BC). They were not only places of worship but also centers for education, social gatherings, and communal support.

The synagogue emerged as an institution to replace the Temple in Jerusalem after its destruction in 587 BC during the Babylonian captivity. In the absence of a centralized Temple, local communities gathered together to study Torah, pray, and offer sacrifices through a practice called "minyan." The synagogue became a way for Jews to maintain their religious identity and traditions while living in diaspora (scattered among other nations).

The services held in the synagogues usually followed a pattern consisting of prayers, readings from the Torah (the first five books of the Hebrew Bible), hymns, and sermons. The Ark, a sacred enclosure for the Torah scrolls, was a central feature in synagogues.

Synagogues were not only spaces for worship but also served as community centers where Jews could discuss local issues, settle disputes, and support one another. They fostered Jewish identity and cohesion among dispersed communities by promoting shared traditions, values, and practices.
