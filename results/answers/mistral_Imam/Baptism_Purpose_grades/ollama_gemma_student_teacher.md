In Christianity, baptism is a significant sacrament that marks the beginning of a person's journey as a Christian. This ritual is rooted in the New Testament and is considered an essential part of Christian initiation.

In the New Testament, Jesus was himself baptized by John the Baptist in the Jordan River (Matthew 3:13-17). This event signified Jesus' acceptance of his mission to begin his ministry and to fulfill God's plan for humanity. The Baptism of Jesus sets a precedent for followers of Christ to be baptized as well.

Baptism symbolizes several things, including purification or the washing away of sins, the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ, and the new life that a Christian has in Him (Romans 6:3-4). It is also an act of obedience to Jesus' command (Matthew 28:19), as well as a public declaration of faith in Jesus Christ.

It's essential to note that practices can vary among different Christian denominations concerning the mode, age at which baptism should occur, and the necessity or optionality of infant baptism. The significance of baptism, however, remains consistent: it is a rite that marks the beginning of a new life in Christ.
