 In the Christian New Testament, the term "saint" has several meanings depending on the context. The most common meaning is a holy person who follows God's commandments and lives a life dedicated to Him. This is reflected in Matthew 19:20, where Jesus tells the rich young man, "If you want to enter into life, keep the commandments." In this sense, all believers are considered saints because they strive to live according to God's will.

Another meaning of saint refers to a person who has died and been recognized by the Church for their exceptional holiness and intercessory powers with God. This recognition often comes after a formal investigation, usually several years or even decades after their death, and is considered canonization in the Roman Catholic Church. In the Eastern Orthodox Church, this process is known as glorification.

It's essential to note that the concept of sainthood in Christianity differs significantly from Islamic ideas about the role of prophets and saints.
