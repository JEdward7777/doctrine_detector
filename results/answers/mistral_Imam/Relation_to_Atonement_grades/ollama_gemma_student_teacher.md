In Islam, there is no belief in a need for propitiation as such because Muslims believe that Allah (God) is always merciful and compassionate.

On the other hand, 'atonement' is often used to describe the process by which a person seeks forgiveness from God after committing a sin. In Islam, this process is typically called 'Tawbah,' or repentance. A Muslim who has sinned sincerely asks for Allah's forgiveness and seeks to make amends for their wrongdoings through various means like prayer (Salah), fasting (Sawm), giving charity (Zakat), and performing good deeds (Amal Saliha).

In some cases, similar to the concept of atonement in Christianity, Islam also has specific acts or expiations (Kaffarah) for particular sins such as breaking a vow, hunting during the sacred months, or having intercourse with one's spouse during menstruation or postnatal bleeding. These acts aim to make up for and seek forgiveness for these wrongdoings.

So while there are similarities in the general sense of seeking forgiveness and making amends for sins, it is essential to understand that 'propitiation' and 'atonement' have distinct meanings in their original religious contexts and may not perfectly translate into Islamic terminology.
