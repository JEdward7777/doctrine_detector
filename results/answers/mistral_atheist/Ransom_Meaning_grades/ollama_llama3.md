 In the Christian narrative, the concept of Jesus as a "ransom" (Greek: "λύτρον", lytrosis) is rooted in the Old Testament understanding of a kinsman-redeemer, who would buy back or redeem property or individuals from slavery or debt.

In the New Testament, particularly in 1 Timothy 2:5-6 and Mark 10:45, Jesus is referred to as the "ransom for all," a metaphor used to express God's love and salvation offered to humanity through Jesus' sacrificial death. This idea suggests that humanity was held captive by sin, and Jesus' death on the cross paid the price (the ransom) to free us from this bondage. It is an expression of the belief that Christ's suffering and death atoned for the sins of humankind, allowing believers to be reconciled with God.

It is essential to note that interpretations of these texts can vary widely among Christian denominations and scholars. Some emphasize the substitutionary aspect, where Jesus takes on humanity's sin and punishment; others focus more on the transformative impact of Christ's life and teachings in offering salvation. This concept should be understood within the larger cultural and historical context of ancient Near Eastern religions and the specific beliefs of the early Christian movement.
