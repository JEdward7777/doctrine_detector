 In a biblical context, the phrase "House of God" often refers to a physical structure or temple that was built as a sacred place for worship and communion with the divine. This idea appears throughout the Old Testament, particularly in reference to the Temple in Jerusalem. However, it's important to note that the concept of 'God's House' extends beyond just a physical building and can symbolize God's presence among his people, or the community of believers themselves.

For example, in Psalm 27:4-5, the psalmist says, "One thing I ask from the LORD, this only do I seek: that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD and to seek him in his temple." Here, the House of God is not just a physical structure but also a metaphor for dwelling with and seeking God.

Similarly, in the New Testament, Jesus speaks of the House of God as a spiritual realm where God dwells (John 2:19-21). This shift in understanding reflects the growing emphasis on faith over physical structures, although the physical temple continued to be significant until its destruction in AD 70.

In summary, the term "House of God" encompasses both a physical structure and a spiritual concept of God's presence among his people or within the individual believer.
